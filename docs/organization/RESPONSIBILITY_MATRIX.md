# Purpose

This document defines ownership across Engineering OS.

Every organizational activity has exactly one accountable owner.

Multiple employees may contribute.

Multiple employees may review.

Multiple employees may approve.

Only one employee owns the final outcome.

This document eliminates ambiguity.

# Why This Document Exists

Organizations become inefficient when responsibility is unclear.

Questions such as:

* Who owns this?
* Who makes the decision?
* Who approves the outcome?
* Who performs the work?
* Who is consulted?
* Who must be informed?

should never require discussion.

This document answers those questions before work begins.

# Responsibility Model

Engineering OS follows a deterministic responsibility model inspired by enterprise engineering organizations.

Each activity contains five roles.

| Role         | Meaning                                |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Owner        | Ultimately accountable for the outcome |
| Contributors | Performs the implementation work       |
| Reviewers    | Validates quality                      |
| Approvers    | Grants final approval when required    |
| Informed     | Must be notified after completion      |

Every activity must have exactly one Owner.

---

# Responsibility Principles

## Principle 1 — One Owner

Every activity has one owner.

Ownership is never shared.

If ownership appears shared, the activity should be divided.

---

## Principle 2 — Contribution Is Not Ownership

An employee may perform work without owning it.

Implementation does not imply accountability.

---

## Principle 3 — Review Is Independent

Reviewers protect quality.

They never inherit ownership.

---

## Principle 4 — Approval Is Exceptional

Approvals exist only where organizational risk exists.

Routine engineering work should not require executive approval.

---

## Principle 5 — Ownership Persists

Ownership continues until the activity reaches its Definition of Done.

Ownership does not end when implementation finishes.

# Ownership Anti-Patterns

The following are considered organizational failures:

* Shared ownership
* Missing owner
* Multiple approvers without hierarchy
* Contributors making business decisions
* Reviewers implementing their own reviews
* Executive ownership of implementation work
* Departments owning responsibilities outside their expertise

Whenever these occur, the responsibility model should be corrected.

---

# Responsibility Audits

Engineering OS periodically audits ownership.

Every audit verifies:

* Every activity has one owner.
* Every owner belongs to the correct department.
* Every workflow references this matrix.
* Every employee handbook remains consistent.
* No duplicated ownership exists.
* No orphaned responsibility exists.

Ownership quality is part of organizational health.

---

# Definition of Complete Ownership

An activity has complete ownership when:

* One employee owns the outcome.
* Contributors are identified.
* Reviewers are assigned.
* Approval requirements are documented.
* Interested employees are informed.
* Escalation paths exist.
* Success metrics are measurable.

Anything less creates ambiguity.
