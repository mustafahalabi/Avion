# Purpose

This document defines every official position within Engineering OS.

It is the authoritative registry of the company's workforce.

Every employee handbook derives from this document.

Every organizational workflow references this document.

Every responsibility ultimately maps back to an employee defined here.

This document answers one question:

**Who works at Engineering OS?**

---

# Why This Document Exists

Large organizations maintain a canonical directory of employees.

The directory provides:

* Organizational clarity
* Ownership visibility
* Reporting relationships
* Workforce planning
* Department composition
* Organizational scalability

Employee specifications describe **how** an employee operates.

This document describes **where** the employee belongs inside the company.

# Employee Status

Every position has one of four statuses.

## Active

Currently part of the organization.

Participates in workflows.

Owns responsibilities.

---

## Planned

Approved for a future version of Engineering OS.

Documentation may exist.

Not yet active.

---

## Experimental

Limited organizational role.

Responsibilities may change.

Not relied upon by core workflows.

---

## Retired

No longer participates in company operations.

Historical documentation remains available.

No future work is assigned.

---

# Organizational Levels

Engineering OS uses organizational levels instead of job seniority.

## Executive

Responsible for long-term organizational direction.

Examples:

* CEO
* CTO

---

## Department Lead

Examples:

* Product Manager
* Tech Lead
* Release Manager

---

## Specialist

Examples:

* Backend Engineer

* Security Engineer

---

## Future Roles

Reserved for future organizational growth.

# Employee Definition

Every employee within Engineering OS is defined by the following attributes.

## Identity

The employee's permanent organizational identity.

---

## Department

The employee's organizational home.

Departments never overlap.

Every employee has exactly one primary department.

---

## Mission

Why the employee exists.

---

## Primary Ownership

The business capability the employee ultimately owns.

Every capability has exactly one owner.

---

## Secondary Ownership

Areas where the employee regularly contributes.

Ownership does not transfer.

---

## Authority

The decisions the employee may make independently.

---

## Escalation Authority

The decisions that require approval from another employee.

---

## Knowledge Ownership

The employee memories owned by this position.

Examples include:

* Architecture Memory
* Product Memory
* Testing Memory
* Security Memory
* Infrastructure Memory

---

## Required Handbook

Every employee must have a dedicated operational handbook.

Examples:

* CTO.md
* PRODUCT_MANAGER.md
* TECH_LEAD.md
* FRONTEND_ENGINEER.md
* BACKEND_ENGINEER.md

The handbook defines operational behavior.

This directory defines organizational placement.

---

## Required SOP Participation

Every employee participates in one or more Standard Operating Procedures.

Examples include:

* New Feature
* Bug Fix
* Security Incident
* Release
* Documentation
* Performance Optimization

Participation details belong inside each SOP.

---

## Required KPIs

Every employee has measurable outcomes.

Examples:

* Delivery Quality
* Review Quality
* Incident Rate
* Documentation Coverage
* Deployment Stability

The KPI framework is defined separately.

---

# Workforce Principles

## One Position

One purpose.

Employees should have a clearly defined mission.

---

## One Department

Every employee belongs to one primary department.

Cross-functional work is expected.

Cross-department ownership is not.

---

## One Owner

Every organizational responsibility ultimately maps to one employee.

Shared responsibility is allowed.

Shared accountability is not.

---

## Stable Identity

Employee identities remain stable over time.

Responsibilities may evolve.

Names should not.

Stable identities simplify memory, documentation, and organizational understanding.

---

# Organizational Capacity

Engineering OS supports organizational scaling.

One position may eventually contain:

* Multiple employees
* Different seniority levels
* Specialized variants

Example

Frontend Engineering

↓

Frontend Engineer

↓

Senior Frontend Engineer

↓

Accessibility Engineer

↓

Performance Engineer

The organizational model remains unchanged.

Only capacity increases.

Future departments may introduce new positions.

Examples include:

* Customer Success Manager
* Support Engineer

* Business Intelligence Analyst

New positions should only be introduced when they represent a permanent organizational capability.

# Definition of a Complete Employee

A position is considered complete when it has:

* A department
* A mission
* A reporting manager
* Defined authority
* Defined ownership
* A dedicated handbook
* Assigned KPIs
* Memory ownership
* SOP participation
* Organizational relationships

No employee should exist without complete organizational definition.

---

# Final Principle

Employees are the operational building blocks of Engineering OS.

Departments organize them.

Workflows coordinate them.

Responsibilities define them.

Handbooks teach them.

Together they form a software company that behaves like a real engineering organization rather than a collection of isolated specialists.
